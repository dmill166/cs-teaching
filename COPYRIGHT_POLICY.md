# Copyright Policy — Third-Party Materials

> **Applies to every public-facing course** in this initiative (and any other public course Dakota produces), for **any** copyrighted material it teaches from — textbooks, articles, datasets, slides, images, video. This is a hard line, not a guideline.

## The rule

**Never reproduce third-party copyrighted content in a public repo.** Our license (CC BY-NC-ND for content, PolyForm Noncommercial for code) governs *our own* work only — it cannot grant any rights to someone else's material, so the safe boundary is: publish only what we authored.

## Always OK

- **Cite and align by reference** — "this unit maps to Zelle Ch. 7," "read §8.3 before class." Referencing a book and structuring a course around its chapters is normal and legal.
- **Teach the underlying concepts** — ideas, facts, and methods aren't copyrightable. Explain loops, recursion, ADTs, normalization, etc. in **our own words and our own examples**.
- **Point learners to the source** — direct students to buy/borrow the book or read the original. The source *is* the resource; we link to it, we don't host it.

## Never OK (without written permission from the rights holder)

- Reproducing the author's **text, figures, diagrams, tables, or photos**.
- Copying **exercise/problem statements verbatim** (the classic textbook trap — end-of-chapter problems are copyrighted).
- **Closely paraphrasing** specific examples or problems (the legal test is "substantial similarity" — write original examples that teach the same idea).
- Redistributing the author's **code/files** (e.g., a textbook's bundled modules) as our own.
- Posting **scans, PDFs, or excerpts** of the material.

## When in doubt

Write it from scratch in our own words with our own example, or cite-and-link instead of reproducing. If a specific reuse seems genuinely necessary, get **written permission** from the publisher/author first, or rely on a documented fair-use analysis — not a guess.

## Why this exists

Captured 2026-06-14 as a permanent rule. Our public repos are portfolio and teaching assets seen by employers, students, and the rights holders themselves; an infringement claim would damage all three goals (teaching credibility, brand, accessibility) at once. The cost of writing original material is trivial next to that risk.
